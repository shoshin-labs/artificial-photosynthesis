We aren’t just capturing energy; we are capturing fuel.

Artificial photosynthesis is the idea of using sunlight to drive chemistry instead of only making electricity. This version goes a bit further: pick the mission, pick the audience lens, and watch the same solar chemistry tell a different story for hydrogen, methanol, or aviation fuel.

What this page is for

This is a polished, shareable static page that explains the idea clearly for the public, policy makers, and curious investors without assuming a chemistry degree. It now behaves like a small cockpit: the controls change the narrative, not just the numbers.

What it is not

It is not a lab notebook, not a claim that the economics are solved, and not a promise that the technology is already cheaper than fossil fuels. The point is to make the pathway legible, while being honest about the proof still missing.

Aviation fuel

The hard mode. It is where artificial photosynthesis has to stop sounding clever and start surviving the cost curve.

  • hard-to-electrify demand
  • high value per unit energy
  • brutal certification bar

Proof gaps

The novel bit is not pretending uncertainty is solved. It is surfacing it.

What still has to prove itself

  • catalyst durability under sun and heat
  • selectivity: making the right molecule, not a soup
  • system engineering at outdoor scale
  • real-world cost versus the fossil route

What the cockpit shows you

The “proof meter” responds to the sliders. It is not a fake KPI; it is a reminder that an elegant molecule still needs an ugly factory, a supply chain, and a maintenance plan.

Roadmap

From lab chemistry to something that can survive outside the lab.

2026–2030

Mission fit

Prove the catalysts survive sunlight, heat, and real-world conditions without falling apart immediately.

2030–2035

Industrial pilots

Integrate AP with heavy-emitting sites so captured CO₂ can become fuel on-site instead of becoming a transport problem.

2035–2040+

Scale-out play

Chase cost parity for hydrogen or liquid fuels so larger solar-fuel facilities can compete with fossil routes.
