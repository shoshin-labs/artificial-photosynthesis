We aren’t just capturing energy; we are capturing fuel.

Artificial photosynthesis is the idea of using sunlight to drive chemistry instead of only making electricity. In the simplest version, light, water, and captured CO₂ become hydrogen, methanol, or aviation fuel that can be stored, shipped, and used later.

What this page is for

This is a polished, shareable static page that explains the idea clearly for the public, policy makers, and curious investors without assuming a chemistry degree. It uses simple language, a clickable reactor model, and a small set of educational controls to show how the system behaves.

What it is not

It is not a lab notebook, not a claim that the economics are solved, and not a promise that the technology is already cheaper than fossil fuels. The point is to make the pathway legible.

Plain-English pathways

Three ways the same light can become something useful later.

Hydrogen

The cleanest starting point. Sunlight helps split water, and the hydrogen can be stored or used as an industrial feedstock.

Methanol

Add captured CO₂ and you can make a liquid fuel that is easier to ship and handle than hydrogen gas.

Aviation fuel

With enough engineering, AP can be part of the synthetic-fuel route for sectors that are hard to electrify.

Roadmap

From lab chemistry to something that can survive outside the lab.

2026–2030

Pilot phase

Prove the catalysts survive sunlight, heat, and real-world conditions without falling apart immediately.

2030–2035

Early adoption

Integrate AP with heavy-emitting industries so sites can capture their own CO₂ and convert it into useful chemicals on-site.

2035–2040+

Utility scale

Chase cost parity for hydrogen or liquid fuels so larger solar-fuel facilities can compete with fossil routes.

Economics, but still readable

The money problem is the bridge from promising chemistry to industrial hardware.

What slows it down

  • catalyst durability
  • reactor engineering at scale
  • heat losses and harsh outdoor conditions
  • the gap between prototype and commercial deployment

Why the project exists anyway

The case for AP is not that batteries disappear. It is that some energy should be stored as fuel, especially where long-duration storage, shipping, or industrial chemistry matter more than electrons alone.
