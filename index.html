What if sunlight made fuel, not just electricity?

Artificial photosynthesis is the idea of using sunlight, water, and captured CO₂ to make useful molecules you can store, ship, and use later. This page keeps it simple: short answers, a few clear charts, and an honest look at what is still hard.

Short version

Solar panels make electricity. Artificial photosynthesis tries to make molecules instead — especially when you want long-duration storage, shipping, or fuels for industries that do not want batteries.

What this page is not

It is not a hype deck. It is not claiming the economics are solved. It is a straightforward explainer for people who want the idea, the tradeoffs, and the current limits.

Questions and answers

One question per card. No decoder ring required.

What is artificial photosynthesis?

It is a way of using sunlight to drive chemistry. Instead of only making power, the system tries to make fuel or feedstock molecules such as hydrogen, methanol, or synthetic hydrocarbons.

Why does it matter?

Electricity is great for lots of things, but not everything. Molecules are better when energy needs to be stored for a long time, moved across distance, or used where liquid fuels or chemical feedstocks are still useful.

What can it make?

The main paths are hydrogen, methanol, and synthetic aviation fuel. Hydrogen is the simplest starting point; methanol and SAF matter when you want liquids and carbon-based fuels.

How does it work?

A simple version is: sunlight gets captured, water gets split, and then the chemistry is steered toward a useful molecule. If you add captured CO₂, the system can make carbon-based fuels instead of only hydrogen.

What is hard about it?

The hard parts are making catalysts last, keeping the reaction selective, surviving outdoor conditions, and doing it all at a cost that beats simpler options.

When could it matter?

First in pilots, then in industrial niches where the product is valuable enough to justify the complexity. If costs fall and durability improves, it could become much more interesting at scale.
